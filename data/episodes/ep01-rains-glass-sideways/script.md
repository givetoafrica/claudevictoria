This is a real planet. Its sky is a beautiful, calm, ocean blue. Astronauts

## 2. THE DOUBT (0:30–2:00)

**NARRATION (Narrator):**
Sixty-four light-years away, in a constellation called the Little Fox,
there's a star just a bit smaller and cooler than our Sun. And hugging that
star — closer than Mercury hugs ours, twenty times closer — is a giant
planet the size of Jupiter.

**NARRATION (small robot, deadpan):**
Blue planet. Big deal. Earth is blue. Neptune is blue. Wake me up when
something violates the laws of physics.

**NARRATION (young explorer):**
But that's the thing — it shouldn't be blue at all. It has no oceans. No
water. It's a giant ball of gas hotter than a pizza oven. A thousand degrees.
So... what's making it blue?

**NARRATION (Narrator):**
That question — asked by real scientists staring at real telescope data —
is where this story stops being pretty... and starts being terrifying.

**VISUAL:** Star map zooming from Earth's night sky into the Vulpecula
constellation; a small orange-tinted star grows; a huge gas giant orbits

curly hair in a white-and-teal spacesuit and a small boxy silver robot with
one large round blue eye watch from a stylized observation deck, the robot
unimpressed, arms crossed.

**ON-SCREEN TEXT:** "No oceans." / "No water." / "So why is it BLUE?"

## 3. ESCALATION 1 — The color that lied (2:00–3:30)

**NARRATION (Narrator):**
In 2013, the Hubble Space Telescope did something never done before: it
measured the actual color of a planet outside our solar system. As the
planet slipped behind its star, the starlight dimmed — and the blue light
dimmed most of all. The missing blue was the planet's own reflected glow.
Cobalt blue. The first true color of an alien world, and it looked just
like home.

**NARRATION (young explorer):**
On Earth, blue means water. Blue means life. Blue means... safe.

**NARRATION (Narrator):**
On this world, blue means something else entirely. The clouds of
HD 189733 b aren't made of water vapor. At a thousand degrees Celsius,
water clouds are impossible. These clouds are made of rock — vaporized,
then condensed into countless microscopic droplets of silicate. And
silicate, when it cools and hardens, has another name you know very well.
Glass. The planet is blue because its skies are full of glass, scattering
blue light exactly the way our air makes our sky blue.

**VISUAL:** Split screen: Earth's soft blue marble vs. the alien planet's
harder cobalt glow; then a dive into the alien cloud deck where shimmering
molten droplets glint like billions of tiny lenses catching the light;
beautiful and wrong at the same time.

**ON-SCREEN TEXT:** "First exoplanet ever color-measured (Hubble, 2013)" /
"Those clouds are GLASS"

## 4. ESCALATION 2 — The wind that breaks the scale (3:30–5:00)

**NARRATION (Narrator):**
Now add the wind. The strongest hurricane ever recorded on Earth reached
about 350 kilometers per hour — strong enough to peel roofs off houses. A
Formula 1 car tops out around 370. A passenger jet cruises near 900. The
winds on HD 189733 b have been clocked at eight thousand seven hundred
kilometers per hour. That is seven times faster than the speed of sound.

**NARRATION (small robot):**
Running the numbers... at that speed you could fly from London to New York
in under 40 minutes. Correction: pieces of you could.

**NARRATION (Narrator):**
Why? The planet is gravitationally locked — one side always faces its star,
roasting forever, while the other side faces eternal night. All that heat
piles up on one side and has nowhere to go... so the atmosphere itself
stampedes around the planet, day side to night side, in a permanent,
planet-wide jet stream. And it drags the glass clouds with it.

**VISUAL:** Speed comparison ladder: a hurricane, a race car, a jet — each
whooshing past scale markers — then the alien wind blasting past all of
them off the chart; cut to the planet with one hemisphere glowing hot
facing its orange star, the other in darkness, arrows of atmosphere
screaming around the equator.

**ON-SCREEN TEXT:** "Hurricane: 350 km/h" / "Jet: 900 km/h" / "THIS PLANET:
8,700 km/h"

## 5. ESCALATION 3 — The rain (5:00–6:30)

**NARRATION (Narrator):**
So let's put it together. Skies full of microscopic molten glass. Winds at
seven times the speed of sound. On Earth, rain falls down. On HD 189733 b,
nothing gets the chance to fall. The glass rain moves sideways —
horizontally — in sheets, at two kilometers per second. Standing on a
platform in those clouds would be like standing inside a sandblaster the
size of a planet. Every surface, polished. Every edge, erased.

**NARRATION (young explorer, hushed):**
And from far away... it just looks like a calm blue marble.

**NARRATION (Narrator):**
The most beautiful thing in the sky, and the most hostile weather we have
ever discovered. The universe does not warn you which one it's being.

**NARRATION (small robot):**
...Okay. I'm awake now.

**VISUAL:** Inside the storm: glowing amber glass shards streaking
horizontally through cobalt-blue cloud canyons, lit by the huge orange star
low on the horizon; then a hard cut to the serene exterior view of the
peaceful blue dot — silence — held for a beat.

**ON-SCREEN TEXT:** "Rain that never falls DOWN" / (on the calm exterior
shot) "This is the same planet."

**Notes:** This is the emotional peak and the rewatch moment. The cut from
chaos to serene exterior is the shot the whole episode is built around.

## 6. THE REVEAL — How we actually know (6:30–8:00)

**NARRATION (Narrator):**
Here's the best part: no spacecraft has ever been there. Everything you
just saw, scientists worked out from a single flickering point of light,
sixty-four light-years away. When the planet crosses its star, a tiny bit
of starlight filters through its atmosphere — and the atmosphere leaves
fingerprints in that light. Sodium fingerprints. Glass fingerprints. When
they measured the heat racing from the day side to the night side, they
clocked the wind. When they watched the blue light vanish behind the star,
they found the color.

**NARRATION (young explorer):**
So it's not a guess. It's a measurement. Detective work — with a telescope.

**NARRATION (Narrator):**
Every wild thing in this episode was read from starlight by people on this
planet who have never left it. Fiction needs an author to imagine it.
Reality just needs someone to look closely enough.

**VISUAL:** Elegant diagram-style sequence: a planet silhouette crossing a
bright star; a beam of starlight passing through the thin glowing rim of
atmosphere and splitting into a spectrum with dark fingerprint lines; a
curious young explorer with dark curly hair in a white-and-teal spacesuit
tracing the spectrum lines in the air while a small boxy silver robot with
one large round blue eye projects the data from its chest.

**ON-SCREEN TEXT:** "No probe. No photo. Just starlight." / "Science =
detective work"

## 7. FACT STACK — It gets weirder (8:00–9:00)

**NARRATION (Narrator):**
Still not convinced this place is showing off? Three more things — all
measured, all real. One: a year there — one full trip around its star —
lasts just two point two Earth days. You'd have a birthday every other day.
Two: in 2024, the James Webb Space Telescope smelled it. Hydrogen sulfide —
the rotten-egg gas. The glass-storm planet also stinks. Three: its star is
slowly boiling it away. The atmosphere is evaporating into space by
thousands of tonnes per second — the planet is beautiful, deadly... and
disappearing.

**VISUAL:** Three rapid vignette cards: a birthday cake with orbits
spinning past it; a cartoon whiff of green stink lines rising off the blue
planet while a small boxy silver robot pinches where a nose would be; the
planet trailing a faint glowing comet-like tail of escaping atmosphere.

**ON-SCREEN TEXT:** "1: A year = 2.2 days" / "2: It smells like rotten
eggs (JWST, 2024)" / "3: It's evaporating"

## Fact checklist (all claims sourced before publish)
- HD 189733 b: transiting hot Jupiter, ~64 ly, constellation Vulpecula. ✔
- First exoplanet with measured visible-light color — cobalt blue (Hubble, 2013). ✔
- Blue from silicate (glass) cloud scattering, not oceans; dayside ~1,000 °C. ✔
- Wind speeds measured ~8,700 km/h (~2 km/s; ~7× speed of sound). ✔
- Tidally locked; orbital period ~2.2 days; ~0.03 AU from star. ✔
- Sodium detected in atmosphere; transmission spectroscopy method. ✔
- H2S (rotten-egg gas) detected by JWST, 2024. ✔
- Atmospheric escape/evaporation observed (X-ray/UV observations). ✔
- "Sideways glass rain" is the standard scientific characterization of its
  weather; framed as inference from measurements, per taboo-topic rule on
  labeling speculation. ✔
